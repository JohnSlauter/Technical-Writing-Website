Restrictions on Social Gatherings

"Louisiana and Georgia were responsible for 14 percent of the United States’ daily coronavirus cases, despite making up less than 5 percent of the country’s population"

Various gatherings across America played a large role in the spread of coronavirus across the US in February 2020. However, most of this spread fell under the category of cryptic transmission, which is unrecognized transmission due to the common presymptomatic and asymptomatic spread of COVID. Other causes of cryptic transmission include limited COVID testing availability and co-occurrence with the transmission of other respiratory diseases. The test first produced by the CDC in February was faulty and it took weeks to fully revise it. Additionally, the introduction of coronavirus to the US coincided with the annual influenza season, so many early cases were dismissed as the flu. The CDC pinpoints 3 events in late February as catalysts for the sharp rise in confirmed cases in early-mid March: Mardi Gras festivals and celebrations across Louisiana with over a million attendees, an international professional conference in Boston with about 175 attendees, and a funeral in Albany, Georgia with over a hundred attendees. While the latter two events may not seem as significant as the former due to their relatively small populations, by March 15, 2020, Louisiana and Georgia were responsible for 14 percent of the United States’ daily coronavirus cases, despite making up less than 5 percent of the country’s population. In fact, Dougherty County, Georgia, where Albany is located, had a cumulative incidence of 1,630/100,000 people, which was one of the highest in the country at the time (Schuchat, 2020). Resultantly, the CDC began advising against social gatherings and recommending that people stay home in mid-March.
